Your work on Lean 4 tactic generation through progressive grounding and scaffolding aligns well with several state-of-the-art prompting and reasoning frameworks. Below is a comparison of your approach with existing techniques and suggestions for potential improvements based on the provided sources.

Comparison with Existing Techniques

Decomposition and Planning: Your use of "milestones" and "planning" directly parallels Least-to-Most Prompting, which breaks down a complex problem into a series of simpler subproblems solved in sequence (see article Least-to-Most Prompting Enables Complex Reasoning in Large Language Models). This also aligns with Decomposed Prompting: A Modular 
Approach for Solving Complex Tasks, where sub-tasks can be delegated to specific prompting-based LLMs or symbolic functions (see article Decomposed Prompting: A Modular Approach for Solving Complex Tasks). Similarly, Plan-and-Solve Prompting: Improving 
Zero-Shot Chain-of-Thought Reasoning by Large Language Models proposes devising a plan to divide tasks into smaller subtasks to address "missing-step errors" (see article Plan-and-Solve Prompting: Improving Zero-Shot Chain-of-Thought Reasoning by Large Language Models). Successive Prompting for Decomposing Complex Questions also emphasizes iteratively breaking down a complex task into a simple task and solving it (see article Successive Prompting for Decomposing Complex Questions).

External Grounding (LSP): Your strongest conclusion—that live compiler and search feedback is critical—is supported by SatLM: Satisfiability-Aided Language Models Using Declarative Prompting. This approach argues that LLMs are better at generating declarative task specifications than imperative steps, leveraging an off-the-shelf automated theorem prover to derive the final answer (see article SatLM: Satisfiability-Aided Language Models Using Declarative Prompting). This mirrors your finding that the model becomes a good implementer once a clear mathematical scope is provided.

Specification-First Generation: Your emphasis on writing a precise specification before coding is similar to Look Before You Leap: Problem Elaboration Prompting Improves Mathematical Reasoning in Large Language Models, which clarifies the problem context before reasoning to improve parsing efficiency (see the article Look Before You Leap: Problem Elaboration Prompting Improves Mathematical Reasoning in Large Language Models). It also relates to the focus in SatLM: Satisfiability-Aided Language Models Using Declarative Prompting on parsing specifications accurately to avoid planning errors (see SatLM: Satisfiability-Aided Language Models Using Declarative Prompting).

Progressive Generation: Your "tight feedback loops" resonate with Progressive-Hint Prompting Improves Reasoning in Large Language Models, which uses previously generated answers as hints to progressively guide the model toward the correct answer (see paper Progressive-Hint Prompting Improves Reasoning in Large Language Models).


Suggestions to Improve Your Paper

Drawing from the sources, here are several directions you could explore to further strengthen your research:

Incorporate Analogical Reasoning: You could try the approach in Large Language Models as Analogical Reasoners or the MetaLadder: Ascending Mathematical Solution Quality via Analogical-Problem Reasoning Transfer framework (see articles Large Language Models as Analogical Reasoners, MetaLadder: Ascending Mathematical Solution Quality via Analogical-Problem Reasoning Transfer). Instead of just providing a specification, you could prompt the model to self-generate relevant analogous problems and their solutions (tactics) before tackling the target task (see article Large Language Models as Analogical Reasoners). Thought Propagation: An Analogical Approach to Complex Reasoning with Large Language Models also suggests that exploring analogous problems can enhance complex reasoning by propagating insights from similar solutions (see article Thought Propagation: An Analogical Approach to Complex Reasoning with Large Language Models).

Implement a "Proposer-Verifier" Loop: While you use the LSP as a verifier, you could formalize this into a multi-role framework like Cumulative Reasoning with Large Language Models. In this setup, a Proposer generates tactic steps, a Verifier (assisted by the LSP) checks them, and a Reporter decides when a complete solution is reached (see article Cumulative Reasoning with Large Language Models).

Autonomous Error Recovery: Explore more advanced self-correction methods. Recursive Decomposition with Dependencies for Generic Divide-and-Conquer Reasoning specifically includes an error recovery mechanism to correct mistakes made in previous steps (see article  Recursive Decomposition with Dependencies for Generic Divide-and-Conquer Reasoning). Similarly, Boosting of Thoughts: Trial-and-Error Problem Solving with Large Language Models uses error analysis from the LLM to explicitly revise subsequent prompts (see articles Boosting of Thoughts: Trial-and-Error Problem Solving with Large Language Models).

Address "Distraction" and Redundancy: You could test how your scaffolding handles irrelevant information. Concise and Organized Perception Facilitates Reasoning in Large Language Models suggests that distilling the most relevant information and organizing the context can significantly elicit better reasoning (see article Concise and Organized Perception Facilitates Reasoning in Large Language Models).

Recursive Decomposition for Scale: If your tactics become very large, you might consider the method in Recursive Decomposition with Dependencies for Generic Divide-and-Conquer Reasoning. When complexity stems from the input length or depth, the task can be recursively broken down into smaller, more manageable inputs (see Recursive Decomposition with Dependencies for Generic Divide-and-Conquer Reasoning).

Your focus on the specification as the hardest step is a compelling insight. You might strengthen your paper by formally comparing "researcher-written specifications" against "model-generated specifications" that have been refined through the LSP, similar to how Language Model Self-improvement by Reinforcement Learning Contemplation uses an LLM to evaluate its own text quality (see article Language Model Self-improvement by Reinforcement Learning Contemplation).
